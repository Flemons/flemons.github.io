Defining an AI Product

Encompassing the first month of my time at integrate.ai, the first pass of the product saw me answer what could an artificial intelligence product look like?

With no other product team members, and no pre-existing product I worked on all aspects of definition, design, and development from scratch.

The project's objective was obtaining a “lighthouse” partner through a pitch of a functioning product that better enables our clients to integrate our AI.

The final presentation succeeded in achieving buy-in from this key partner, resulting in a long-term contract with us and a solidified partnership that still holds strong today.

Company

integrate.ai

Date

August 2018 – September 2018

Project Duration

4 Weeks

My Roles

Product Designer
UX Researcher
Front-End Developer

Problem

Over 50% of integrate.ai’s clients operated on short-term contracts due to our lack of tangible product and difficulty of attributing our impact on their business.

Solution

A web-based application that enabled users to visually see the impact integrate.ai as well as their business constraints, had on their results.

How did I get here?

Getting context

Focus

With no background in artificial intelligence, I dedicated my efforts to understanding the domain. Retaining as much as possible about what the AI, I sought to answer:

  • What does our AI accomplish today?
  • How does it do this?
  • Who are our buyers?
  • What problems were they having?
  • Where was the opportunity for an AI product to aid them?

Approach

  • Two one-on-one interviews with advocates from this client
    Asking a broad spectrum of foundational questions around the users, their processes, and why they believed in the IAI product.
  • Interview loop with multiple internal domain experts
    Used as proxies for users from a wide range of roles.
  • Support Audit
    Utilized the Customer Love team to highlight common support tickets and hacks they had created.

Outcomes

To consolidate the research, a rough persona and 3 key challenges they face were identified:

  • Lack of transparency - There was little understanding as to how the AI works. People were apprehensive about taking direction from it.
  • Difficulty in tracking - The users only had their own internal tracking tools, making it difficult to prove our impact on their outcomes.
  • Lack of control - A model requires 2-3 revisions before implementation due to changing client needs. This was a massive engineering effort and impacted the strength of the models.

Proposal

With the 3 distinct problem areas identified, a pitch was completed to gather feedback from the leadership team. The main outcome of an upsell still top of mind, we identified the Lack of control as the biggest opportunity for us.

It would be a bigger industry “first”, potentially leading to more outside interests from users and had the potential to alleviate the other issues depending on implementation.

Exploring possibilities

Inspiration

The majority of my inspiration came from a wide range of other industries. I utilized my domain experts for inspiration, then constructed a broader product inspiration board.

This combined their suggested inspiration, along with a large swath of inspiration from transportation, big data, investing, sci-fi, and vintage tech.

Wireframing

WIth the inspiration board as a starting point, the focus was on novel concepts in sketching, keeping them simple to quickly finish as many concepts as possible.

I scheduled daily meetings with the product owner formatted as pitches the ideas to get pointed feedback on what worked, and what didn’t for further refinement.

After pitching a select few concepts to the domain experts, the decided approach was to have a visual illustrating predicted outcomes of users choices, in the spectrum of other predicted possibilities.

From my research, the users identified are constantly comparing, looking for better ways to improve their work which best fit with this idea.

Low-fidelity mock-ups

Iteration here focused on different ways to layout the controls and the data visualization, as well as integrating any necessary information for each model.

On top of my regular check-ins with the product owner, I pulled more information from my domain expert group to better prioritize the information hierarchy of the product.

Mid-fidelity mock-ups

After a final touch base with the product owner for feedback, 4 concepts which had been shown off were chosen to be refined.

A discussion with the domain experts led to the line graph being chosen would work best due to its simplicity and striking appearance.While the other options had more detail available, for the project's purpose it benefited from having less as the final pitch would be short.

High-fidelity mock-ups

The final mockup consisted of 2 key components, the left Constraints input, and the right Expected Lift line graph output.

The Constraints input in the UI was a challenge as the user is making decisions that could impact millions of dollars in revenue. The inputs played a part in this understanding, with big but simple choices to make at each input.

The Expected Lift graph utilized bright vibrant colouring, with animated line movements, drawing the eye to the changes in predicted outcome, clearly demonstrating the comparison of the model to model changes, and summarized with a single metric outcome.

Coding it into reality

As the only front-end developer, my experience was in HTML/CSS and Javascript, but engineering had a preference for React.js due to its ease of manipulation with new models, so I learned and coded in React.js instead.

Collaborating closely with engineering, I researched and identified a library for the visualization (chart.js) that was react native, and implemented it along with a user input panel using react components.

This developed proof of concept was the main focuses of our final pitch.

How things ended up

The presentation was set with 6 decision makers from our client, 3 at an executive level. I introduced, pitched and then demoed our products' functionality directly to the client’s. The majority of the buyers were immediately intrigued, with the rest joining after a Q&A period.

The clients were excited to see more, and had lot’s of immediate feedback on functionality, process, data, and layout.

The project was deemed a success as within a few weeks they had grown their contract with us, solidifying them as our ”lighthouse” partner.

Learnings

Given the speed of the project and my like of tenure at the company, I took the objectives given to me at face value and questioned very little, overestimating my capabilities. This led to a larger workload, and rushed portions of the project.

With only a few weeks to deliver, it would have been beneficial to have a more in depth initial discussion on the results the team hoped for outcomes of the project, and where my skill set could be applied to best achieve it.

The other main takeaway from this project was the importance of time boxing. With the short time span, my calendar should have been immediately scoped to what needed to be done, in order to keep a gauge on my progress, and identify sticking points.
