Bonci

A better app for finding restaurants

Brief

The purpose of the project was to identify a market sector that needed improvement and innovation.

The app selection for restaurant browsing at the start of the projects process was poor, leaving a large market segment that could be improved.

The project was finalized with a mockup displaying the finalized interface, as well as a key-path scenario to show a potential users personal experience through the app.

Problem

A buyout and subsequent cancellation of a major app in the restaurant reviewing field left a large untapped market.

Competitors in the field at the time left much to be desired. With common issues of low app functionality, too much and poorly laid out information, as well as frustrating navigation, a new take was needed.

Solution

A new application was designed to solve this problem. Bonci provides a simple, straightforward interface that encourages users to review more often through incentive.

Additionally, content limitation is key for the application, to ensure loading speed and memory use are kept to a minimum.

Design Requirements

The BONCI app came to amalgamation when presented with the task of identifying a viable segment or product that needs to be redesigned. The product needed to be a pre-existing piece, or opportunity for a product that could be implemented better than what is currently available.

From personal experience, it was decided that the current restaurant rating apps available were poor quality. The previous app I used daily for my restaurant research needs called “Urbanspoon” was bought out by a newer competing app “Zomato”. At the time of starting this project, “Zomato” has just been released, and it showed in the quality of the app. Disjointed menu’s, hard to read content, it was extremely difficult to use. Stemming from here I researched from other previous users, and users of similar apps their experiences.

Research

Using the friends and family I knew had interactions with the Urbanspoon app, I devised a series of FIVE questions that would yield me qualitative and quantitative data for interpretation.

With these questions in place I got into contact with the friends and family and organized interview times. After getting five users opinions from varying demographics, the data was set up for comparison. Although each user had unique answers, their was some consistencies about their personal habits when using the application and others. A visual was created corresponing with the questions in order to show the correlation between the users better.

With the replies gauged visually, I could make conclusions from the data I had.

Their were a few that seemed most important to the design of the app. Firstly, users wanted to spend an minimal amount of time using the application. The users surveyed all leaned towards the making quick choices option, except for one outlier.

Another interesting point found in the data collected was that the majority of these users are using it travelling. Going back to the idea of having quick access, if a user is travelling, they may want to have access to the nearby examples quickly. This coupled with the data point that all of the users were eating new things means that the majority of the users are using these apps when finding something in a location they may be unfamiliar with.

Finally, the majority of the users would not spend anytime writing reviews. They would only use the applications and websites for browsing the currently available establishments. This has the potential fora big problem for a new app, as it is completely dependant on individuals to leave reviews.

Personas

In order to better explore the minds of the users, I used the data I collected and conclusions I made to construct personas. The goal of this was to encapsulate all the frustrations of the end user into demographics that best reflected the data I found.

Wireframes

Using the findings from my interviews, I did a few rough mock-up's of the layout of my app. First I decided to test with a right aligned bar. This kept more restaurants visible when viewing the map. Working with this design I created another iteration, including the content that I had determined was most important for decisions for users. This is where I realized the amount of content was too great for constraining the information to the side of the screen. It became a nightmare for me to cram everything into the small area. It also made it slightly more difficult for users using their left hand, with the menu further away

Next I tried a simple 4 element layout. The map took up the majority of the screen space, with the restaurant "blocks " underneath. This was a good familiar baseline for me to build off of, so I continued to work with it by adding the information needed for completion.

I found the content was much more legible and easier to layout when allocated to the bottom of the page instead. The map was also larger, making it easier to pick out street names and restaurant locations, especially for quick reference.

Key-Path Scenario

With the layout in mind and mocked up, a key path scenario was created to show the advantages to the new interface, as well as to identify any problem areas that may arise. This key path scenario follows the previously created persona Greg to show his mind-set going through the app.
