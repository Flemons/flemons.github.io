Exploring Solutions to Road Rage

At the end of summer 2020, Canada has started to return to normal from COVID-19. Coincidentally, road rage incidents have been in the news nearly every day for the past few weeks.

A recent study in Canada found that 66% of road users admitted to “minor aggression” on the roadway, with 2% surveyed admitting to physical assault. If presentative, this means that 200,000 people in Canada are willing physical harm in these disputes. [i]

In this ongoing personal project, I am looking to see what I can create either in a digital or physical medium, that can alleviate or negate the effects of road rage.

Currently I am conducting secondary research through fly on the wall analysis, and contextual inquiries.

Background

Being an avid auto enthusiast growing up, I believed that the road was filled with poor drivers and was adamant not to become one. Fast forward a few years and I am driving home from Toronto and experience an aggressive road rage incident first hand. This man had put himself and others in danger with his poor driving, and to achieve nothing. I wanted to know, why did he lose control and act this way? And equally important, how can I help others to not act the same way?

Secondary Research

Using available articles and scholarly documentation available on the web. With assumptions and bias from my own personal experience, grounded myself with as much context from varying resources as possible.

What is road rage?
Although not a legal term in Canada, I defined road rage as an exertion of anger in the context of driving, predominantly aimed towards other road users. This can vary in outcome, from swears or gestures thrown to actual acts of violence but all occur behind the wheel. [i] [ii]

What causes road rage?
The research here varies from study to study with no specific clear cause as this domain is not actively researched. Some common examples of underlying problems I found in my research were:

  • Traffic congestion and slow progress in travel
  • External stress
  • Distractions
  • Tailgating
  • Isolation and perceived anonymity
  • Lack of control
  • Lack of humanization
  • Underlying mental health issues

The inciting incident that results in road rage comes from driving behaviours that you see everyday. Getting cut off, near misses, no use of turn signals, speeding, driving too slowly, distracted driving. All of these and more could be the mistake that drives people to act.[i] [ii] [iii] The other key insight I found was that road rage causes more road rage. Like a domino effect, when one person road rages, it is likely the victim will do the same.[i]

Who is most affected by road rage? Who is most targeted by road rage?
Younger males aged 16-19 are the perpetrators of the most road rage in the U.S. according to the NHTSA, with those in the millenial age bracket (1981-1996) accounting for over 50% of all aggressive driving accidents.[i] This changes from men to women when comparing those who have 4+ instances of road rage per week.

Another study in China found income impacts people's response to driving. Those who were in lower income “levels”, exhibited more aggressive and dangerous habits to incidents.[i]

Some other interesting learnings:

  • A lot of the frustrations in these situations have their basis in the purpose of the personal transportation to begin with.”...driving was a potent source of autonomy ... meanings that were often frustrated by the travails of negotiating the road system”[i]
  • Men were found to be both the main perpetrators (92%), and victims (96%) of these events
  • The most common age range for both aggressors and victims from a newspaper study was around 33 years old
  • Drugs and alcohol were found to be attributed to no road rage incidents in recent years [i]

What has been tried?

  • Anger management training
  • Breathing exercises
  • Better driver education [i]
  • Auditory distractions (music/podcasts) [i]
  • Visual reminders (photos of loved ones)
  • Leave earlier/with no rush
  • Sleeping more [i]
  • Relaxation training
  • Cognitive therapy
  • Better trip planning [i]

There has been a lot of success from these solutions, especially those around teaching and preventative measures. One study saw an overall improvement among 90% of those included. [i] However, it was noted in the same study and others that it is difficult to get someone to realize they are a road rager.

Active approaches such as calmer music, and breathing exercises have shown to reduce reactions in active situations, but only if a driver recognizes what is happening. The red mist is the biggest blocker for these solutions.
