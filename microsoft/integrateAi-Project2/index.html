Generalizing a specialized product

With the first iteration of the integrate.ai product under our belts, it was clear we were going to need to test the product, and attempt to apply the product towards other verticals with a new approach.

This iproject focused on redefining our understanding of users through collaborative research, and developing a live product users could engage with. I collaborated with external researchers, my new Director of Design, and designed the new iteration of the product.

The end result was a product that had been released to 3 of our major partners and a roadmap to make it more widely accessible in the near future.

Problem

The initial focus of effort towards one specific company resulted in a product too specialized to be applied elsewhere.

Solution

This revision of the integrate.ai product reworked the initial concept by iterating the forecasting tool and adding two new products to complete our defined model lifecycle.

Defining today and scoping tomorrow

Testing what we have

We conducted multiple interview sessions, divided between foundational questions for role understanding, and then qualitative usability testing for feedback on the user flow.

To consolidate the research, a rough persona and 3 key challenges they face were identified:

  • Lack of transparency
    Little understanding of how the AI works meant people were apprehensive about taking direction from it.
  • Difficulty in tracking
    The users only had their own internal tracking tools, making it difficult to prove our impact on their outcomes.
  • Lack of control
    A model requires 2-3 revisions before implementation due to changing client needs. This was a massive engineering effort and impacted the strength of the models.

A lot of these issues had basis in the higher level problems we had heard before: trust, transparency, and control. These would be the focus of the new iteration.

Getting external help

Post our internal research effort, we decided we would need external assistance to define our understanding of our users.

We worked with a independent researcher who focused on our existing client base. My role here saw me onboarding the researcher as well as participating in their user interviews.

We then moved to a larger agency to broaden our understanding through external companies. At this stage, I engaged in interviews and ran workshops on research results.

In conclusion of the research, we had an understanding of 2 personas involved in our focused process, along with the foundations of a third persona to be refined later.

Collaborating with the ML team

Accompanying the research, I conducted a product review with the machine learning team for feedback on the use cases affect on the product. This identified problem areas for focus in the upcoming ideation, and the use case implications.

Two major challenges identified were:

  • Forecasted predictions for each model has decreased
    Due to the ambiguity in visitors to the website, and has to be based off of historic visitation trends
  • Far clearer understanding of impact
    As the AI was now closed loop, when a user was influenced by our AI, it could be easily attributed.

Identifying a user flow

I completed a suggested user flow to guide the rest of the design of the product to serve as a baseline to identify any gaps in the product before undertaking any ideation effort.

This utilized the current understanding of our clients process with defined problems that had been raised in this round of research.

Making the product tangible

Looking elsewhere for ideas

Instead of pulling from more abstract and futuristic looking interfaces, inspiration was pulled from a wide spectrum of currently existing products.

This led me to ecommerce sites such as Apple, Google, and IKEA with their approach to guiding users to a final product, matching the defined user flow.

Exploring what fits our users problem

In these sketches I explored concepts in ecommerce as well as industries like package tracking/logistics, music streaming, and search even some SQL tools.

With my Director, and using the internal domain experts, we came to the conclusion of using the ecommerce focused product.

The comparison from one result to the next was far more clear, and it’s flow held more familiarity for the new users on the platform.

Making it real

Forecast was the focus of the product, allowing users to pick the campaign that fits their needs. It worked with two new components Activate (campaign management) and Monitor (campaign tracking).

Forecast
Comparing models in forecast was key, so the inclusion of bookmarking and comparing was a critical addition. This was with more metrics and visually scannable bars displaying the impact the promotions would have.

The visual changes to forecast suited the shift to real-time inbound as the visuals relied on percentages meaning it would scale as users edited their search results.

Activate
After a user had selected their model, there needed to be a next step to monitor, modify, or end models. The solution was Activate, which lists all models selected by users. Inspiration came from music queues where current, previous, and upcoming songs were organized in a scannable list.

Activate better reflected the pre-existing process with ‘following’ campaigns as time progresses. What Activate improves is a summary view, all activate, queued, and past campaigns in a digestible page.

Monitor
A major piece of feedback from the initial product was a lack of deep dive potential. The users are used to tableau dashboards or deep excel spreadsheets.

Using tableau as inspiration, this product formed into Monitor. I did not replicate their dashboards. Instead, I determined what functionality Monitor could benefit our users.

How things ended up

Demoing a more usable thing

The end product was demonstrated to our big three partners who had immediate interest and we transitioned to implementing the application for them as quickly as possible.

This fully developed product became the center of all future pitches, being demoed to clients across North America. With it, our sales team managed to get interest from multiple new companies, ultimately leading to two new major contracts signed.

Learnings

The importance of properly testing

Despite resulting in multiple opportunities for our company, in the spirit of speed the next step of testing was never completed. With a quick transition to the next step, the major changes and tweaks for usability were never validated

While business objectives were completed successfully, a strong validation plan for design objectives was never completed, resulting in the lack of focus from the broader team post launch.

The importance of a validation plan is especially important in non-design focused companies as it can drive our immediate action beyond implementation.
